Rowing works your whole body, but it gives your core an especially good workout.

 

Your abs may not work alone like in a sit-up, but they're important for every part of the rowing stroke.

### **How Your Abs Power the Rowing Stroke**

 

**The Catch:** At the start of the stroke, your abs flex to coil your torso slightly forward, positioning you for optimal power generation.

 

**The Drive:** While the drive primarily focuses on legs and back, your abs are constantly engaged to stabilize your core and transfer power smoothly.

 

**The Finish:** Abdominals remain contracted to maintain a strong, upright posture as you complete the stroke.

 

**The Recovery:** During the slide back forward, your abs actively flex your torso to prepare for the next powerful catch position.

 

This might be a good time to show what your abs are actually made of.

_Infographic - Ab Anatomy_

 

Now that you know where each of your ab muscles are, lets find out what they do in the rowing stroke:

 

**1. Linea Alba:** Acts as a core stabilizer, especially noticeable during the powerful drive phase.

 

**2. Tendinous Inscriptions:** Provide abdominal support, not moving but helping maintain structure throughout the stroke.

 

**3. Serratus Anterior:** Assists in stabilizing the upper body during the finish and early recovery, aiding in torso and arm positioning.

 

**4. External Oblique:** Crucial for rotation and stability during the drive and finish, enabling powerful strokes.

 

**5. Internal Oblique:** Works with external oblique for rotation and stability, particularly active during the drive to maintain a solid core.

 

**6. Transversus Abdominis:** Acts like a corset, ensuring posture and stability throughout all rowing phases, facilitating efficient power transfer.

 

**7. Rectus Abdominis:** Key for spinal flexion, moving from the finish to the catch, and stabilizing the core across the rowing cycle.

 

**8. Inguinal Ligament:** Provides lower abdominal support, keeping the lower core engaged during the rowing movement.

 

### **Can You Get Visible Abs From Rowing**

 

I know you want a strong, defined core. And rowing definitely works your abs, but there's more to getting visible abs than just rowing.

 

A 2008 study published in the Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise journal shows that rowing activates nine muscle groups. Your abdominals being one of them, making the rowing machine a great tool for building muscle [[1](https://journals.lww.com/acsm-msse/fulltext/2008/03000/physiological_and_performance_effects_of_low_.26.aspx)].

 

To see those abs, you need to reduce body fat. [Rowing is great exercise](https://rowingwithwatts.com/what-muscles-does-a-rowing-machine-work/) and can help burn calories. But eating healthy matters quite a bit.

 

A 2011 study showed that doing ab exercises alone won't get rid of belly fat [[2](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21804427)]. 

 

You are in luck though, A 2015 study also suggest regular rowing can contribute to overall fat loss [[3](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4564707/)].

 

Rowing won't give you bodybuilder-style abs by itself. You need targeted ab exercises that will help you build more defined muscles. 

 

However, rowing is a fantastic way to get leaner, which helps those ab muscles pop!

In contrast, someone new to indoor rowing with a lower fitness base might gain both muscle and lose fat.

 

## Tips to engage your core on the rowing machine

 

Engaging your core effectively on a rowing machine enhances the workout benefits. It reduces the risk of injury.

 

Here are tips to ensure proper core activation during your rowing sessions:

| Tip | Description |
| --- | --- |
| Maintain Good Posture | Sit straight, avoid slouching for proper core engagement. |
| Set Your Feet Properly | Secure feet on the footplates with straps for a solid core foundation. |
| Start With Core Engagement | Tighten abdominal muscles before starting strokes to maintain engagement. |
| Smooth Drive Phase | Begin strokes by driving with the legs, keeping the core engaged to stabilize the upper body. |
| Control the Recovery | Use the core to control movement back to the start to prevent collapsing. |
| Proper Breathing | Sync breathing with strokes—inhale during recovery and exhale during the drive. |
| Emphasize Hip Action | Focus on hip movement, swinging from slightly past vertical to vertical and back during strokes. |
| Avoid Using Feet to Pull Up | Relying on strapped feet can lead to poor core engagement and hip pain. |
| Row with Feet Out of the Footplates | Row without strapping feet to enhance core activation, forcing more active body stabilization. |
| Practice Feet-Out Rowing | Use feet-out rowing during warm-up to improve core engagement and perfect stroke sequence. |
