I rowed 10,000 m every day for 30 days and tracked all the data from the rowing machine, my daily health stats and my diet.

 

To get the most comprehensive view of the impact from rowing on my body, I did before and after body assessments that ended up giving some unexpected results.

 

These assessments included:

 
 
- Photos 
- Biometric evaluation 
- Data on vital biomarkers 
- ECG analysis 
- Data on my heart/autonomic nervous system. 
   Key Takeaways        

I lost **13 lbs / 5.9 kg**

 

I burned **19,858 Calories **

 

My 10k time improved by **9 minutes 22 seconds**

 

I completed **300,000 meters** on the Rowing Machine

   

## Before starting the challenge

 

_[Patellofemoral pain](https://www.mayoclinic.org/diseases-conditions/patellofemoral-pain-syndrome/symptoms-causes/syc-20350792#:~:text=Patellofemoral%20(puh%2Dtel%2Do,that%20involve%20running%20and%20jumping.), _that's how the idea behind this challenge started. Also known as runner's knee.

 

I had run my first marathon and had a niggle in my knee that would not go away. The physiotherapist recommended taking a break and doing low-impact exercises for a while.

 

I had done a few sessions on the rowing machine before, 500 m sprints and a 2k row for time. So I decided to challenge myself and complete 10,000 m every day for 30 days and document the whole journey.

 

Definletly not what my Physio had in mind.

 

Little did I know how much I would fall in love with indoor rowing.

### InBody

 

The [InBody](https://inbody.com/en) assessment provides a thorough analysis of your weight, body composition, height, and BMI. 

 

When you stand on the machine and hold the handles, it runs a weak electrical current through your body. This allows it to measure the currents changes known as Bioelectrical Impedance Analysis.

### PNOĒ

 

The [PNOĒ](https://pnoe.com/) test measures breathing data, using a reliable method known as _[ergospirometry](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9068094/)_.

 

Its the gold standard for professional athletes.

 

Testing 23 biomarkers that assess vital areas, including:

 
 
- Metabolism 
- Cellular function 
- Heart & lung fitness 
- Fat-burning efficiency 
- Biological age. 
 

![Tarquin Stapa doing a PNOE Test. A man wearing a mask on his head during a rowing session.](/uploads/2024/01/Tarquin-Stapa-doing-a-PNOE-Test-1024x576.webp)

 

One thing though, I am no professional athlete (as you will see by some of my scores).

 

Great data on where my fitness levels were at before I started this indoor rowing challenge.

#### Windows of Trainability

 

A visual representation of my body's ability to train across four main areas. Endurance, Speed & Power, Strength, and Coordination & Skill.

 

When those four principals are green, it states my body is ready and able to do hard training. Since three are yellow and one red, I should consider modifying training intensities.

 

I can still train if its red, it would just come at at greater physiological cost.

 

#### Overall Readiness

 

My readiness score before the rowing challenge was at 4.0, which is fair.

 

The Functional State data reveals my central nervous system (CNS) was fatigued at level 1.0.. The CNS manages my body's activities. Intense training and heavy lifting strain it.

 

My cardiac system (CS) was ready to go at 7.0.

 

The Omegawave data recommended my heart rate training only go up to Aerobic Development (Z4). 

 

Rowing is an aerobic workout so great to hear.

 

![Omegawave - Initial Assessment. Cardiac System. Rowing Machine Challenge](/uploads/2024/01/Omegawave-Initial-Assessment.-Cardiac-System.-Rowing-Machine-Challenge-1024x768.webp)

#### Autonomic Balance

 

The sympathetic nervous system triggers the fight or flight response in danger. While the parasympathetic nervous system calms the body afterwards. Both are in the green range for me.

### Weekly Experience

 

I documented each day's rowing experience on YouTube. Feel free to go watch all the highs and lows of the journey on our [Rowing With Watts Channel](https://www.youtube.com/@RowingWithWatts).

 

#### Week 1 (Day 1-7)

 

The first week was all about getting used to being on the rowing machine for extended periods of time. The longest distance I had done up to this point was a 2k. Now I was doing 10,000m per day and my body was freaking out a bit.

 

Numb bum and legs, blisters on my hands and the final 1000m felt like hours to complete.

 

As the days passed, my technique improved and my comfort level increased. Each session brought progress in speed and endurance.

 

#### Week 2 (Day 8-14)

 

I found more of a rhythm in the second week that blended improvement with consistency. My focus on diet and energy paid off, leading to better performance.

 

I was more present some days than others, but finishing each 10k gave a sense of accomplishment.

 

This week only reinforced my determination. It heightened my excitement as I approached the halfway mark of the challenge.

 

#### Week 3 (Day 15-21)

 

This is when I started to get into the flow of indoor rowing. So often meters would melt away.

 

Great start by hitting the halfway mark, and adapting well to the demands of rowing. My energy levels fluctuated, but I stayed committed, even setting a new personal best by Day 21.

 

I felt a stronger connection with the rowing process. Balancing the challenge of pushing myself with maintaining my well-being.

 

#### Week 4 (Day 22-28, + 29)

 

I was in my stride in this final week. Meters would fly by. Bum and leg was not getting numb anymore. Hands were fine. My mind could wonder while maintaining rowing stroke pace. 

 

I really enjoyed being on the rowing machine at this point. The sound of the fan became therapeutic. I was however taking it easy, looking to conserve my energy for day 30.

 

#### Final Day (Day 30)

 

Was really looking to set a good time here. Sub 40min was my goal. Pushed as hard as I could and emptied my tank but ended up just missing it. A final time of 40min 41sec. So close!

[Garmin's Aerobic Training Effect (TE)](https://www.garmin.com/en-US/garmin-technology/running-science/physiological-measurements/aerobic-training-effect/) refers to the excess post-exercise oxygen consumption (EPOC) during a workout. Measured on a scale from 0 to 5, it considers your fitness level and training routines.

 

Aerobic exercise is continuous activity using oxygen. While anaerobic exercise is short, intense activity using muscle energy.
