## Arm Anatomy – Your Levers Of Strength In Rowing

 

So you are sitting on your rowing machine, pulling on the handle and wondering just how hard your arms are actually working. 

 

Well, your arms contribute around 10% during the rowing stroke. Legs 60% and your body the remaining 30%.

 

We made the infographic below to break down all of the muscles in your arms that activate during the rowing stroke.

Now that is a gun show right there.

 

The numbers below correspond to their respective muscles in the infographic above.

 

### Biceps

 

You engage your biceps when you pull the handle towards your chest during the drive phase, helping to bend your elbows efficiently.

 

Biceps muscles Involved: 

 
 
- 1 - Biceps Brachii  
 

### Triceps 

 

You use your triceps to extend your elbows and stabilize your arms during the finish phase, as you push the handle away to prepare for the next stroke.

 

Triceps muscles Involved: 

 
 
- 2 - Triceps Brachii 
 

### Deltoids

 

You activate these shoulder muscles throughout the rowing stroke, primarily during the drive and finish, to control the movement of your arms and ensure a powerful posture.

 

Deltoid muscles Involved: 

 
 
- 3 - Anterior Deltoids 
- 4 - Medial Deltoid 
- 5 - Posterior Deltoid 
 

### Forearms

 

You rely on your forearm muscles to maintain a firm grip on the handle throughout the stroke, ensuring precision and control with each movement.

 

Forearm muscles Involved: 

 
 
- 5 - Flexor Carpi Radialis,  
- 6 - Palmaris Longus, and  
- 7 - Flexor Carpi Ulnaris

Lets break down what your arms are doing a bit further.

 

### **How Your Arms Power the Rowing Stroke**

 

**The Catch:** Your arms are extended, with biceps and forearms engaged to grip the handle.

 

**The Drive:** As you push back, your biceps and latissimus dorsi pull the handle towards your body.

 

**The Finish:** At the end of the stroke, your biceps fully contract, and your trapezius and rhomboids ensure a strong finish.

 

**The Recovery:** As you return to the catch position, your triceps straighten your elbows, and your forearms maintain grip for the next stroke.

## Tips to engage your Arms on the rowing machine

 

Your arms are the final piece of the rowing stroke. Here are some tips help ensure you are getting the most from the last bit of energy in the indoor rowing stroke.

| Tip | Description |
| --- | --- |
| Light Grip on the Handle | Avoid gripping the handle too tightly. This helps prevent over-engaging your arms and reduces energy loss. |
| Keep Arms Extended | Maintain long, extended arms during the drive phase for efficient power transfer from legs to the handle. |
| Avoid Bending Elbows Early | "When the arm bends, the power ends." Bending elbows early disrupts power transfer from legs to handle. |
| Engage Lats | Allow shoulders to reach forward and engage lat muscles to maintain a strong leg-to-arm connection. |
| Snap at the Finish | Use a quick, controlled snap of your arms at the end of the stroke, focusing arm movement primarily here. |
| Relax Hands and Shoulders | Keep hands relaxed and shoulders loose throughout the stroke to avoid premature arm fatigue. |
| Release and Extend | After the snap, quickly release arms back to the extended position for recovery, preparing for the next stroke. |
| Focus on Legs and Hips | 60% of power comes from legs, 30% from hips, and only 10% from arms. Maximize leg and hip drive. |
